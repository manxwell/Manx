Part 1. Ethics in Ancient Philosophy

Plato, Euthyphro

Plato’s Euthyphro – Overview

  • Question: What is piety?

    • Greek: piety = τὸ ὅσιον,to hosion

  • (Dictionary definition of ‘piety’: the quality or virtue of proper reverence or devotion

    – towards the gods/God, towards one’s parents/family, etc.)

  • Divisions:

    • Prologue (p. 35-37/2a-5d)

    • First Definition (p. 37-38/5d-6e)

    • Second Definition (p. 38-40/6e-9d)

    • Third Definition (p. 40-42/9d-11d)

    • Fourth Definition (p. 42-44/11d-14b)

    • Fifth Definition (p. 44/14b-15c)

    • Epilogue (p. 44-45/15c-d)

  • A short and sweet introduction to Socrates, Plato, Ancient philosophy, ethics, and philosophy in general.

    • What is piety? $\to$ What is the good life? How should we live?

Plato’s Euthyphro – Prologue: What is piety? (p. 35-37/2a-5d)

  • Socrates and Euthyphro are both on the way to the court-house.

  • Socrates informs Euthyphro that he has been indicted for corrupting the youth and impiety towards the gods (35)

  • Euthyphro is prosecuting his own father for the murder of one of his day-laborers. (36)

    • The father then bound the laborer and placed him in a ditch while he waited for the authorities. But in the mean-time, the laborer died.

    • Note: Euthyphro’s actions would have been considered a violation of one’s duties towards one’s father.

    • But Euthyphro insists that everyone should prosecute a murderer, whenever someone is killed unjustly, no matter who.

    • Ethical question: Do we have special duties towards our family-members? Or should we treat them exactly the same as everyone else?

  • Euthyphro’s relatives say that it is impiousfor him to prosecute his own father. But Euthyphro disagrees. “You see, it’s impious, they say, for a son to prosecute his father for murder. Little do they know, Socrates, about the gods’ position on the pious and the impious!” (37/4d-e)

  • Socrates declares he will enlist Euthyphro to teach him about the meaning of piety, so that Socrates can defend himself against the charge of impiety.

Euthyphro’s First Definition of Piety (p. 37-38/5d-6e)

  • Euthyphro’s first definition of piety: “I say that what’s pious is precisely what I’m doing now: prosecuting those who commit an injustice, such as murder or temple robbery, or those who’ve done some other wrong, regardless of whether they’re one’s father or one’s mother or anyone else whatever. Not prosecuting them, on the other hand, is what’s impious.” (37/5d-e)

    • Euthyphro’s proof by example: Zeus imprisoned his own father Kronos in the Underworld. And Kronos castrated his own father Uranus. So even the gods recognize that one must punish one’s own fathers, when necessary.

    • Socrates are suspicious that the gods are really like this...

  • Socrates’ Objection: Euthyphro has merely offered an example of something pious (namely, prosecuting one’s father for an injustice). But Socrates wants to know what piety itself is.

    • “Do you remember, then, that what I urged you to do wasn’t to teach me about one or two of the many pieties, but rather about the form itself, by virtue of which all the pieties are pious? You see, you said, I believe, that it was by virtue of one characteristic that the impieties are impious, and the pieties pious. Or don’t you remember? ... Then teach me what that characteristic itself is, in order that by concentrating on it and using it as a model, I may call pious any action of yours or anyone else’s that is such as it, and may deny to be pious whatever isn’t such as it.” (38)

    • Philosophical Point: This is what we search for in philosophy. Philosophy isn’t about accumulating examples. It’s about concepts of things, definitions of things, ideas of things. (The ideas of, e.g. piety, justice, truth, goodness, beauty, etc. Or: The Forms. )

Euthyphro’s Second Definition of Piety (p. 38-40/6e-9d)

  • Euthyphro’s second definition of piety: “In that case: what’s loved by the gods is pious, and what’s not loved by the gods is impious.” (38/7a)

  • Socrates’ first objection: The gods disagree among themselves about what is good or bad, i.e. what to love or not to love. So the gods disagree about what is pious and impious. (38-39)

    • Euthyphro’s response: But the gods all agree on at least some things. For example: All the gods hate murder, and so murder is clearly impious, and the prosecution of a murderer is pious.

      • Socrates’ response: But the gods may disagree over whether a given action falls under the category ‘murder’. For example: The case of Euthyphro’s father – his father’s killing of a day-laborer who he bound and placed in a pit while waiting for the authorities – seems open to disagreement.

  • Socrates: Even if we grant that Euthyphro’s prosecution of his father is pious, what have we really learned about piety by saying that it is ‘what the gods love’? Why do the gods love this?

Euthyphro’s Third Definition (p. 40-42/9d-11d)

  • Euthyphro’s third definition of piety: “All right, I’d say that the pious is what all the gods love, and its opposite, what all the gods hate, is the impious.” (40/9e)

    • Socrates’s question: “Is the pious loved by the gods because it’s pious? Or is it pious because it’s loved by the gods?” (40/10a)[The Euthyphro Dilemma!]

    • Option #1: If the pious is loved by the gods because it is [already] pious, then it is pious independently of the gods. Piety pre-exists the gods’ love. And so the question remains: What is piety? What makes something pious?

      • Example: Suppose murder is impious independent of the gods’ love. But why is murder impious, then?

    • merely depends on what the gods happen to love. (And presumably the gods could have loved something else, which means that the pious is arbitrary.) So the question remains: Why do the gods love this and not that?

      • Example: Suppose murder is impious because the gods hate it. But why do they hate it?

        And if they had instead loved it, would that have made murder pious?

    • (Another monotheistic version of the dilemma in terms of God and ‘rightness’: Is what is right/good commanded by God because it is right/good? Or is it right/good because it is commanded by God?

      • If what is right is commanded by God because it is right, then ‘rightness’ exists independentlyof God (God is just the messenger, and is bound by independent standards – therefore not all-powerful). But then the question remains: What is right? What makes something right?

      • If what is right is right merely because God commanded it, then rightness is arbitrary (God could have commanded something else). And the question remains: Why did God command this and not that?)

The Euthyphro Dilemma

Euthyphro’s Fourth Definition of Piety (p. 42-44/11d-14b)

  • (The Euthyphro Dilemma has shown that being loved by the gods is merely a quality of the pious, not its proper definition.)

  • Fourth definition: The pious as a whole is what is just. (42)

  • Socrates’ question: Are ‘pious’ and ‘just’ synonymous? Is everything pious also just, and everything just also pious?

    • Euthyphro concedes that piety is just a part of justice. Socrates asks him which part of justice piety is.

    • Euthyphro: Piety is about justice with respect to the gods, while the other parts of justice pertain to relations with human beings. Specifically, piety is about ‘tending’ to the gods, i.e., providing them with a certain ‘service’.

      • Socrates: What sort of ‘service’ does piety render to the gods?

      • Euthyphro: It’s hard to give an exact answer. But it has to do with offering the proper prayers and sacrifices. Such prayers and sacrifices help preserve the welfare of the household and of the city. (43-44)

Euthyphro’s Fifth Definition of Piety (p. 44/14b-15c)

  • Fifth definition: Piety is “some sort of knowledge of sacrificing and praying” (44/14c)

    • Praying is about asking from the gods, sacrifice is about giving to the gods.

      • Socrates: We ask the gods for what we need, and we give to them what they need? So piety would be “a sort of expertise in mutual trading between gods and men”? (44/14e)

      • Euthyphro: Yes.

      • Socrates: But don’t we receive everything from the gods? And aren’t the gods in need of nothing? What could we possibly give the gods?

      • Euthyphro: We can give the gods honor and reverence. Honor and reverence are pleasing to the gods. To give the gods what is pleasing to them is piety.

      • Socrates: But why is this pleasing to the gods? Because the gods love it? But this simply brings us back to the earlier problematic definition of piety as ‘what the gods love’. We’ve gone in a circle!

Epilogue (p. 44-45/15c-d)

  • Socrates: “So we must examine again from the beginning what the pious is, since I won’t willingly give up until I learn this. Don’t scorn me, but apply your mind to the matter in as many ways and as fully as you can, and then tell me the truth – for you must know it, if indeed, any human being does, and, like Proteus, you mustn’t be let go until you tell it. For if you didn’t know with full clarity what the pious and the impious are, you’d never have ventured to prosecute your old father for murder on behalf of a day laborer. On the contrary, you wouldn’t have risked acting wrongly because you’d have been afraid before the gods and ashamed before men. As thing stand, however, I well know that you think you have fully clear knowledge of what’s pious and what isn’t. So tell me what you think it is, my excellent Euthyphro, and don’t conceal it.” (44-45/15d)

    • [Heavy Socratic irony!]

  • Euthyphro: “Some other time, Socrates. You see, I’m in a hurry to get somewhere, and its time for me to be off.”

  • Socrates: “What a way to treat me, my friend! Going off like that and dashing the high hopes I had that I’d learn from you what things are pious and what aren’t. Then I’d escape Meletus’ indictment by showing him that Euthyphro had now made me wise in religious matters, and ignorance would no longer cause me to improvise and innovate about them. What’s more, I’d live a better way for the rest of my life.”

    • [Socrates would go on to be executed for impiety.]

Aporia in the Platonic Dialogue

  • As in many of Plato’s early dialogues, the Euthyphro ends in aporia– literally an “impasse”, more generally a state of “puzzlement” or “confusion”.

  • In essence, aporia means that the question under consideration (in this case, “What is piety?”) has not been satisfactorily answered.

  • Is aporia a bad thing? Sometimes in philosophy, asking the right question is more important than finding the answer.

    • And in fact, haven’t we learned a great deal about what piety is not over the course of the dialogue? This is very helpful information!

  • (But don’t worry: in Plato’s Republic – one of Plato’s middle dialogues – Socrates will answer the question “What is justice?”)





Plato, Apology; Crito; Phaedo (115b-118a)

Plato, Apology

Recap of Previous Lecture (Plato’s Euthyphro)

  • Socrates is charged with (1) corrupting the youth, and (2) impiety towards the gods.

  • Socrates asks Euthyphro to answer the question “What is piety?” on the way to the courthouse.

  • None of Euthyphro’s answers are ultimately coherent or satisfying (due to the Euthyphro Dilemma and other associated problems).

  • Socrates is obliged to face the charge of impiety without having learned from Euthyphro what piety really is!

Plato’s Apology – Background

  • In Greek, ἀπολογία (apologia) does not mean ‘saying sorry’. Rather it means ‘a speech in defense’ of something.

    • Plato’s Apology is an account of Socrates’ speech in defense of himself against the charges of which he has been accused – (1) corrupting the youth, and (2) impiety towards the gods of the city.

  • Breakdown of the Apology:

    • Opening Statement (p. 46-48/17a-20e)

    • Socrates and the Delphic Oracle (p. 49-51/20e-24b)

    • Defense against the Two Charges (p. 51-54/24b-28b)

    • Socrates on Life and Death (p. 54/28b-29b)

    • Socrates the Gadfly (p. 54-56/29b-31c)

    • Socrates on Politics and Oratory (p. 56-59/31c-35d)

    • Socrates’ Sentencing (p. 59-61/35e-38b)

    • After Sentencing (p. 61-63/38b-41c)

    • Socrates’ Final Statements (p. 63/41c-42a)

Socrates’ Opening Statement(p. 46-48/17a-20e)

  • Socrates says that he is not a ‘clever’ speaker. But he will speak the truth. (46/17a-c)

    • Socrates has never spoken before the courts, despite being 70 years old. He asks the jury not to be offended if he speaks as he does in the marketplace.

  • Socrates says that he has been slandered in public, for example by the comic playwright Aristophanes, whose play the Clouds depicts Socrates as a corrupter of the youth. (47/18b-d)

    • The play mounts the following accusation: “Socrates commits injustice and is a busybody, in that he investigates the things beneath the earth and in the heavens, makes the weaker argument the stronger, and teaches these things to others.”

    • In other words, the play depicts Socrates as a sophist– a paid teacher of rhetoric in Athens, who teaches one how to argue their case before the Assembly or the Courts, even if one is in the wrong.

      • But Socrates does not charge anyone. And he teaches the truth, not mere rhetorical persuasion.

    • Socrates recognizes that the jury may wonder how it is that he has come to be slandered in this way. Mustn’t he have been doing something wrong, in order to have acquired this bad reputation? Socrates will explain the story. (48/20c-d)

Socrates and the Delphic Oracle (1)(p. 49-51/20e-24b)

  • One day, Socrates’ friend Chaerephon asked the Oracle at Delphi whether anyone in Athens was wiser than Socrates. The Oracle replied that Socrates was the wisest person in Athens. (49/21a)

  • When Socrates heard this, he thought there must be some mistake. For Socrates never thought he had any wisdom to speak of.

  • So Socrates decided he would work to prove the Oracle wrong. He would go to the marketplace and speak with the truly wise men of Athens, and in hearing the wisdom they have, Socrates would show that he was not the wisest.

  • But when Socrates went to speak to people, and asked them to share their wisdom by answering questions like “What is piety?”, “What is justice?”, “What is virtue?”, and so on, he found that people’s answers didn’t make sense. These people didn’t actually know the things they claimed to know.

  • “And when I examined him and talked with him, men of Athens, my experience was something like this: I thought this man seemed wise to many people, and especially to himself, but wasn’t. Then I tried to show him that he thought himself wise, but wasn’t. As a result, he came to dislike me, and so did many of the people present. For my part, I thought to myself as I left, “I’m wiser than that person. For it’s likely that neither of us knows anything fine and good, but he thinks he knows something he doesn’t know, whereas I, since I don’t in fact know, don’t think that I do either. At any rate, it seems that I’m wiser than he in just this one small way: that what I don’t know, I don’t think I know.” Next, I approached another man, one of those thought to be wiser than the first, and it seemed to me that the same thing occurred, and so I came to be disliked by that man too, as well as by many others.” (49/21c-e)

    • Often summarized: ‘The only thing I know is that I know nothing.’

      • [Consider the example of Euthyphro!]

Socrates and the Delphic Oracle (2)(p. 49-51/20e-24b)

  • Though Socrates’ questioning was arousing hostility, he continued on his quest, in reverence of the god of the Oracle.

    • Socrates does not possess any special wisdom about the things he asks other about (e.g. piety, justice, virtue, the good). Rather, he simply reveals that others do not have this wisdom. It is as if the Oracle were to say: “That one among you is wisest, mortals, who, like Socrates, has recognized that he’s truly worthless where wisdom’s concerned.” (50/23b)

    • “So even now I continue to investigate these things and to examine, in response to the god, any person, citizen, or foreigner I believe to be wise. Whenever he seems not to be so to me, I come to the assistance of the god and show him that he’s not wise. Because of this occupation, I’ve had no leisure worth talking about for either the city’s affairs or my own domestic ones; rather, I live in extreme poverty because of my service to the god.” (50/23b)

  • Young people like to follow Socrates around to hear him question others.

    • Some think Socrates is thereby corrupting these young people. But he is doing no such thing. They are only witnessing the pursuit of wisdom and truth.

Socrates’ Defence Against the Two Charges(p. 51-54/24b-28b)

  • Two charges against Socrates: “Socrates is guilty of corrupting the young, and of not acknowledging the gods the city acknowledges, but new daimonic activities instead.” (51/24b)

  • First Charge: Corrupting the Youth

    • Socrates demonstrates to his accuser Meletus that no one does evil intentionally – everyone does what they believe to be good. So if Socrates is doing evil by interacting with the youth in the way he does, he must be doing such evil unintentionally (for Socrates believes it to be good, not evil). But then Socrates shouldn’t by punished, but rather instructed by Meletus on what is truly good. (52/25d-26a)

    • Famously summarized: ‘Ignorance is the root of all evil.’

  • Second Charge: Impiety towards the Gods

    • Meletus claims that Socrates is corrupting the youth by denying the reality of the gods.

    • But Socrates points out that the indictment contradicts this. The indictment says that Socrates has introduced ‘new daimonic activities’. But daimons are a kind of god, or children of gods. So Socrates clearly does not deny the existence of all gods, since he acknowledges the existence of his ‘new’ daimons. [Note: Daimon does not mean ‘evil demon’. It simply means any superhuman spiritual being.] (53/27a-e)

Socrates on Life and Death(p. 54/28b-29b)

  • Socrates is willing to stake his life on his philosophical activity:

    • “But perhaps someone may say, “Aren’t you ashamed, Socrates, to have engaged in the sort of occupation that has now put you at risk of death?” I, however, would be right to reply to him, “You’re not thinking straight, sir, if you think that a man who’s any use at all should give any opposing weight to the risk of living or dying, instead of looking to this alone whenever he does anything: whether his actions are just or unjust, the deeds of a good or bad man.” (54/28b) [Ethical question: Are some values worth dying for?]

  • Just as Socrates stood his ground in battle, he will stand his ground in philosophizing.

    • Indeed, he has been appointed to this station by the god. If he were to give it up out of fear for his own life, that would be true impiety towards the gods.

    • For all we know, death may be a passageway to a better life. But we know for certain that acting badly in this life is wrong.

Socrates the Gadfly(p. 54-56/29b-31c)

  • If Socrates were to be released on condition that he cease from practicing philosophy, he would refuse. (55/29c-d)

  • Socrates is in fact giving a great service to the city: “I believe that no greater good for you has ever come about in the city than my service to the god. You see, I do nothing else except go around trying to persuade you, both young and old alike, not to care about your bodies or your money as intensely as about how your soul may be in the best possible condition” – that is, about justice, piety, virtue, goodness, etc. (55/30a-b)

    • Killing Socrates will in fact harm the city of Athens much more than it will harm Socrates.

    • Socrates provides the service of the “gadfly” of the city of Athens – one who ‘awakens, cajoles, and reproaches’ the citizens in the service of the cause of justice, virtue, and goodness.

    • Clearly Socrates does not perform this service for his own sake. He has neglected his own affairs for years, for the sake of helping others to grow in knowledge and wisdom, without ever asking for payment.

Socrates on Politics and Oratory(p. 56-59/31c-35d)

  • Why did Socrates not participate in the Assembly [the democratic legislative body of Athens]?

    • Socrates has a ‘daimon’ – a voice that only he can hear – that instructs him on things to avoid. The daimon told him to avoid public discourse, but instead to focus his philosophical activities on private conversations. (56/31c-e)

  • Socrates did challenge political authority on multiple occasions:

    • Socrates was the only one to vote against an unjust group prosecution of naval soldiers after a battle, despite threats of punishment on himself for doing so. (Group prosecution was known to be unjust.)

    • Under the reign of the Thirty Tyrants, Socrates refused to arrest Leon of Salamis and bring him before the tyrants to be executed, despite the danger this posed to Socrates himself. (56-57/32c-d)

  • Socrates will not break down in tears, or bring his wife and children before the jury, in order to try to elicit sympathy for his plight. He prefers to simply present the justice of his case. (58-59/34b-35d)

    • If Socrates did try such rhetorical tricks, he would be encouraging the jury to violate their own oath to uphold justice.

      That would be true impiety towards the gods.

Socrates’ Sentencing (p. 59-61/35e-38b)

  • Offstage, Socrates is convicted. Meletus demands the death penalty. Socrates is given the right to offer a counter-proposal for his penalty.

  • Socrates says that he has only tried to offer the “greatest benefaction” to the city, by encouraging its citizens to become the best and wisest people they can be. Thus his proper ‘punishment’ should be free meals in the Prytaneum! (59-60/36b-37a) [Socratic humor!]

    • Socrates is convinced that he has does no injustice. So it would be unjust if his proposed a true punishment for himself.

  • Socrates asks: Should he propose to pay a fine? Imprisonment? Exile?

Socrates’ Sentencing (p. 59-61/35e-38b)

  • Fine: Socrates does not have the money to pay.

  • Imprisonment: Socrates does not want to be enslaved to the prison-guards. (He knows imprisonment will be bad, whereas death may be good.)

  • Exile: Socrates would run into the same trouble wherever he went.

  • “Now perhaps someone may say, “But by keeping quiet and minding your own business, Socrates, wouldn’t it be possible for you to live in exile for us?” This is the very hardest point on which to convince some of you. You see, if I say that to do that would be to disobey the god, and that this is why I can’t mind my own business, you won’t believe me, since you’ll suppose I’m being ironical. But again, if I say it’s the greatest good for a man to discuss virtue every day, and the other things you’ve heard me discussing and examining myself and others about, on the ground that the unexamined life isn’t worth livingfor a human being, you’ll believe me even less when I say that. But in fact, things are just as I claim them to be, men of Athens, though it isn’t easy to convince you of them.” (60/37e-38a)

  • Socrates says he would be happy to pay a fine if he had the money, since he doesn’t care about money.

    • Socrates’ friends (including Plato) intervene, proposing a fine of thirty minas.

Quote

The unexamined life is not worth living. ~Socrates

After Socrates’ Sentencing(p. 61-63/38b-41c)

  • Offstage, Socrates is sentenced to the death penalty.

  • Socrates wants the jurors to know that he could have used all sorts of shameful means to persuade them to acquit him, but that he would rather face death than incur such shame – just as a soldier would rather die in battle than shamefully surrender to the enemy.

    • Though Socrates faces death, his accusers face the far worse punishment: namely, the damage to their soul brought about by their own vice and injustice.

  • Socrates prophesies that he will not be the last philosopher. Many more people like him will rise up in his place. Philosophy cannot be destroyed by killing one man.

  • Socrates on death:

    • If death is simply a long sleep, then it is a welcome respite from the suffering of life, and does not harm the one who sleeps.

    • If death involves a migration to the afterlife, then it is a welcome journey, for one will come to converse with all the great wise and heroic people who have come before. (e.g. Homer, Hesiod, Odysseus, etc.)

    • Even in the afterlife, Socrates would engage in his characteristic activity of asking people about their wisdom, as he did in life.

Socrates’ Final Statements (p. 63/41c-42a)

  • “But you too, gentlemen of the jury, should be of good hope in the face of death, and bear in mind this single truth: nothing bad can happen to a good man, whether in life or in death, nor are the gods unconcerned about his troubles. What has happened to me hasn’t happened by chance; rather, it’s clear to me that to die now and escape my troubles was a better thing for me. It was for this very reason that my sign never opposed me. And so, for my part, I’m not at all angry with those who voted to condemn me or with my accusers. And yet this wasn’t what they had in mind when they were condemning and accusing me. No, they thought to harm me – and for that they deserve to be blamed.” (63/41c-d)

  • “This small favor, however, I ask of them. When my sons come of age, gentlemen, punish them by harassing them in the very same way that I harassed you, if they seem to you to take care of wealth or anything before virtue, if they think they’re someone when they’re no one. Reproach them, just as I reproached you: tell them that they don’t care for the things they should and think they’re someone when they’re worth nothing. If you will do that, I’ll have received my own just deserts from you, as will my sons.” (63/41e-42a) [Socratic humor!]

  • “But now it’s time to leave, I to die and you to live. Which of us goes to the better thing, however, is unclear to everyone except the god.” (63/42a)

Main Points and Questions

  • Main Points:

    • Two charges: corrupting the youth and impiety towards the gods

    • Socrates is the wisest person in Athens because he alone knows that he knows nothing (whereas other people think they know various things, but don’t)

    • Socrates as the ‘gadfly’ of the city

    • The unexamined life is not worth living.

      • Socrates is willing to face death rather than give up philosophizing, which he regards as a life project in the service of justice, virtue, goodness, etc. Socrates as a martyr for philosophy.

  • Questions:

    • Was Socrates guilty of the two charges against him? (Should philosophy corrupt the youth and be impious towards the gods?)

    • Is the unexamined life worth living?

    • How would Socrates fare in today’s world?





Plato, Apology; Crito; Phaedo (115b-118a)

Plato, Crito

Plato’s Crito – Background

  • A ‘sequel’ to Plato’s Apology

  • Depicts a conversation between Socrates and his friend Crito, while Socrates is imprisoned, awaiting execution.

  • Breakdown:

    • Crito’s Arguments for Socrates’ Escape (p. 64-66/43a-46a)

    • Socrates’ Arguments Against Escape (p. 66-68/46a-50a)

    • Socrates’ Conversation with the Laws (p. 68-71/50a-54d)

    • Socrates’ and Crito’s Final Words (p. 71/54d-e)

Crito’s Arguments for Socrates’ Escape(p. 64-66/43a-46a)

  • Crito visits Socrates in jail, informing Socrates that he will be executed in the next day or two. (p. 64)

  • Crito exhorts Socrates to take his advice and escape from prison, with Crito’s help. Socrates has thus far refused to escape.

    • Crito declares that if Socrates is executed, not only will Crito lose his friend, but other people will think that Crito was too miserly/cheap to use his money to bribe the guards to allow Socrates to escape. This will bring shame on Crito. (p. 65)

      • Socrates replies that Crito shouldn’t care what people will think of him. He should only be concerned with doing the right thing.

    • Crito asks whether Socrates is refusing to escape because he is worried that his friends who help him escape will face punishments from the Athenian authorities.

      • Socrates replies that this is not what is stopping him from escaping.

  • Crito ensures Socrates that he will be able to find friends or friends-of-friends to take care of him in other cities, if Socrates escapes and goes into exile. (p. 65)

  • Crito argues that Socrates would effectively be abandoning his own sons if he refuses to escape from execution, and this would be a failure of his responsibility towards them. (pp. 65-66)

  • If Socrates doesn’t escape, it will seem like either he or Crito or both are cowards. (p. 66)

Socrates’ Arguments Against Escape(p. 66-68/46a-50a)

  • Crito has claimed that if Socrates does not escape, the majority will come to have a bad opinion of Crito (for failing to help his friend escape). Socrates replies that we should only be concerned with the opinions of the wise (who know what is truly good and bad), not with the majority. (p. 66)

    • Just as an athlete trusts the opinion of a doctor or trainer for the sake of the well-being of the body, we should trust the opinion of the wise person for the sake of the well-being of the soul (which is improved by justice, and harmed by injustice). (p. 67)

  • Socrates asserts that the most important thing is not merely living, but living well. For Socrates, living well means living justly. So him and Crito must examine whether escaping would be the just thing to do. (p. 67)

    • It would be unjust to reward those who have wrongly sentenced Socrates to death by paying them bribe money. (p. 67)

    • Socrates and Crito agree that it is always wrong to intentionally commit an injustice. This includes the injustice of repaying wrong with another wrong (‘Two wrongs don’t make a right.’)

      • But escaping would somehow involve wronging the city and its Laws, by reneging on Socrates’ agreement to follow them. (p. 68)

      • To explain this point, Socrates engages in an imaginary conversation with the personified Laws of the city of Athens.

Socrates’ Conversation with the Laws (1)(p. 68-71/50a-54d)

  • The Laws ask Socrates: “Tell us, Socrates, what do you intend to do? Do you intend anything else by this act you’re attempting than to destroy us Laws, and the city as a whole, to the extent that you can? Or do you think that a city can continue to exist and not be overthrown if the legal judgments rendered in it have no force, but are deprived of authority and undermined by the actions of private individuals?” (p. 68/50a-b)

    • Socrates suggests that he might reply that he intends to destroy an unjust legal judgment that has been pronounced against him.

    • The Laws reply: The agreement to be subject to the Laws of the city requires adhering to legal judgments, whether or not one agrees with them. (A legal system in which individuals are permitted to disregard any judgments they take to be incorrect would not be a legal system at all.) [Ethical question: Are the Laws right about this?]

  • The Laws remind Socrates that it was they who brought his father and mother together through the laws of marriage. It was the Laws who educated Socrates through the laws of education. (p. 69)

    • So just as one should have respect for one’s parents and teachers, one should have respect for the Laws.

    • One is free to try to persuade the Laws to act differently. But if this fails, one must respect the result.

Socrates’ Conversation with the Laws (2)(p. 68-71/50a-54d)

  • The Laws declare that if Socrates had ever been dissatisfied with their handling of any issue in the city, he was always free to leave and seek out a different city with different laws. But if you have stayed here all this time, then you have agreed to be subject to the Laws. (p. 69)

    • Socrates himself has been the most devoted Athenian. He lived his whole life in Athens, and visited other cities less than any other Athenian (only for a single festival, and for his military service). Socrates agreed to raise his children in Athens. So clearly he has been satisfied with the Laws of the city.

  • The Laws remind Socrates that at his trial he turned down the punishment of exile, and was willing to accept his own death in order to avoid injustice. (p. 70)

Socrates’ Conversation with the Laws (3)(p. 68-71/50a-54d)

  • The Laws foresee the following bad consequences if Socrates were to escape:

    • Socrates’ friends will risk being punished for helping him.

    • Socrates will be regarded as an outlaw in the city to which he escapes.

    • Socrates will confirm his accusers’ view that he is an evil man (one who breaks the law will be seen as a corrupter of the youth).

    • Socrates would not be able to honestly philosophize in a city to which he escapes, praising virtue and justice – for he himself will have acted unlawfully.

      • Socrates could give up his philosophizing and just live out the rest of his days eating and sleeping. But this isn’t a life worth living for him. (p. 71)

  • The Laws advise Socrates that his children should not be raised in exile, and assure him that his children will be no better taken care of in Athens by his friends whether he escapes to Thessaly (i.e., goes into exile) or escapes to Hades (i.e., is executed). (p. 71)

Socrates’ Conversation with the Laws (4)(p. 68-71/50a-54d)

  • The Laws’ closing statement:

    • “No, Socrates, be persuaded by us who reared you. Don’t put a higher value on children, on life, or on anything else than on what’s just, so that when you reach Hades you may have all this to offer as your defense before the authorities there. For if you do do that, it doesn’t seem that it will be better for you here, or for any of your friends, or that it will be more just or more pious. And it won’t be better for you when you arrive there either. As it is, you’ll leave here—if you do leave—as one who has been treated unjustly not by us Laws, but by men. But suppose you leave, suppose you return injustice for injustice and bad treatment for bad treatment in that shameful way, breaking your agreements and commitments with us and doing bad things to those whom you should least of all treat in that way—yourself, your friends, your fatherland, and ourselves. Then we’ll be angry with you while you’re still alive, and our brothers, the Laws of Hades, won’t receive you kindly there, knowing that you tried to destroy us to the extent you could. Come, then, don’t let Crito persuade you to follow his advice rather than ours.” (p. 71/54b-d)

Socrates’ and Crito’s Final Words(p. 71/54d-e)

  • Socrates: “That, Crito, my dear friend, is what I seem to hear them saying, you may be sure. And, just like those Corybantes who think they are still hearing the flutes, the echo of their arguments reverberates in me and makes me incapable of hearing anything else. No, as far as my present thoughts go, at least, you may be sure that if you argue against them, you will speak in vain. All the same, if you think you can do any more, please tell me.” (p. 71/54d)

    • [Question: Is Socrates saying that he agrees with the Laws’ arguments? Or that he has merely been ‘charmed’ by them, as the Corybantes are charmed by the sounds of the flute?]

  • Crito: “No, Socrates, I’ve nothing to say.”

  • Socrates: “Then, let it be, Crito, and let’s act in that way, since that’s the way the god is leading us.”

Plato’s Crito – Questions

  • Was Socrates right to refuse to escape?

    • Recall that Socrates had disobeyed authority before: he refused to arrest Leon of Salamis when ordered by the Thirty Tyrants to do so. (But perhaps he regarded the rule of the Tyrants as illegitimate.)

    • In any event, may we still admire Socrates for giving his life for what he believed to be just and right?

  • Compare to Aristotle:

    • Legend has it that, decades later, Aristotle was likewise going to be brought up on charges of impiety in Athens.

    • Aristotle decided to flee the city before he could be charged, “lest the Athenians should sin twice against philosophy.”

      • (Note: Aristotle was not an Athenian citizen, but a resident alien or metic.)





Plato, Apology; Crito; Phaedo (115b-118a)

Plato, Phaedo

  • [We are just reading Socrates’ death scene, 115b-118a, which is the very end of the dialogue. Most of the dialogue is devoted to a discussion of the immortality of the soul.]

  • Crito asks: What are Socrates’ final instructions? (p. 72)

    • Socrates’ replies: For Crito and his friends to live well.

  • Crito asks: How would Socrates like to be buried? (p. 72)

    • Socrates’ replies: In whatever way you think is best. For in truth, it will not be Socrates who is buried, but only Socrates’ (former) body – a corpse. Socrates himself will be long gone.

  • The narrator Phaedo describes the feelings of all those present: Socrates’ death was a great misfortune for them, as if they were losing their father and so being orphaned. (p. 72)

  • Socrates says goodbye to his wife and two sons. (p. 72-73)

  • The guard who brings Socrates the poison hemlock declares that Socrates is “the most decent, gentlest, and best man who’s ever come to this place,” but he must nevertheless do his duty, and hopes Socrates won’t begrudge him for it. (p. 73/116c)

  • Socrates declines to delay his execution; he refuses to drag it out any longer than necessary. (p.73)

  • Socrates asks and receives instructions for how to consume the hemlock. He offers a prayer to the gods for a fortunate journey to the next world, and drinks it. (p. 73)

    • Socrates’ friends who are present all proceed to break down in tears.

    • Socrates chastises his friends for their shameful display of despair. He would prefer ‘reverent silence’.

  • Socrates’ final words: “Crito, ... we owe a cock to Asclepius. Please, don’t forget to pay the debt.” (p. 74/118a)

    • Asclepius was the god of health. Offerings were made to Asclepius after one had been cured from a disease. Socrates seems to be saying that, in dying, he has been cured of the disease of life (or the sufferings of his life). Socrates is quite possibly being bitterly ironic and humorous here.

  • “Such was the end of our friend, ... a man who, we would say, was the best of all those we’ve experienced and, generally speaking, the wisest and most just.” (p. 74/118a)





Part 1. Ethics in Ancient Philosophy

Plato, Republic, Books I and II

Plato’s Republic – Background

  • Plato’s early, middle, and late dialogues

    • Early: Ends in aporia – no answer to the question (e.g. Euthyphro)

      • More representative of the historical Socrates

    • Middle: Questions receive answers. (e.g. Republic answers ‘What is justice?’)

      • Thought to be expressions of Plato’s own views, through the mouthpiece of Socrates.

      • Answers relate especially to the theory of Forms (e.g. the Form of Justice, the Form of the Good, etc.)

    • Late: Complications of answers given in middle dialogues

  • Title of Republic

    • Greek: Politeia – meaning ‘polity’

    • Latin translation: Res Publica

  • Central question of the Republic: What is justice (dikaiosune)?

    • Both justice in the individual soul (what is it to live justly?) and justice in the city (what is it to organize a political system justly?)

    • In the course of answering that question, Plato’s Republic also digresses into other issues of ethics, politics, metaphysics, epistemology, aesthetics, etc.

    • We hear a lot of different things about ‘justice’ today. So it seems to me that Plato’s Republic is just as relevant today as it was 2500 years ago.

  • Also: an extended argument that justice is desirable for its own sake.

  • The Republic is a magisterial work of philosophical genius and inexhaustible profundity, expressed with characteristic exuberant energy and narrative verve.

  • Characters:

    • Socrates: a philosopher

    • Glaucon: Plato’s brother

    • Adeimantus: Plato’s brother

    • Cephalus: an old rich merchant

    • Polemarchus: the son of Cephalus

    • Thrasymachus: a sophist

  • Outline of Books 1 and 2

    • Book 1:

      • Prelude: What is justice?

      • First Definition of Justice: Cephalus

      • Second Definition of Justice: Polemarchus

      • Third Definition of Justice: Thrasymachus

    • Book 2:

      • Glaucon’s Intervention (incl. Ring of Gyges)

      • Adeimantus’ Intervention

      • The Healthy City (City of Pigs)

      • The Feverish City (the titular Republic)

Plato’s Republic, Book 1

Prelude: What is justice?(p. 75-77/327a-331b)

  • On his way back from a festival at the Piraeus, Socrates bumps into Polemarchus and his friends, who invites Socrates back to his home. (p. 75)

    • Polemarchus’ father Cephalus, an old and rich merchant, is there.

    • Cephalus is happy to see Socrates. Socrates asks Cephalus whether old age is a difficult time of life.

  • Cephalus discusses his old age (p. 76)

    • Some of Cephalus’ old friends complain about having lost their sexual appetites, etc. But Cephalus, following Sophocles, is happy to have been freed from such a ‘deranged and savage master’.

    • Cephalus remarks that, if one lives moderately, than both youth and old age are easy to bear. But if one lives immoderately, neither are easy.

    • Socrates asks whether the reason Cephalus finds old age easy to bear is because he is rich?

      • Cephalus replies: A good man may find poverty hard to bear. But a bad man will not be contented even with great wealth.

  • Socrates asks Cephalus what has been the greatest good he has enjoyed on account of his wealth. (p. 77)

    • Cephalus explains that the thing old men fear most is the punishment they may receive in the afterlife (Hades) in retribution for the injustices they committed while living. Cephalus’ wealth has allowed him to avoid committing injustices like cheating, lying, not paying one’s debts, etc. (Cephalus acknowledges that wealth does not automatically help everyone in this way, but only good people – bad people may use wealth for injustice.)

First Definition of Justice – Cephalus(p. 77/331b-d)

  • Socrates: “A fine sentiment, Cephalus. But speaking of that thing itself, justice, are we to say it is simply speaking the truth and paying whatever debts one has incurred? Or is it sometimes just to do these things, sometimes unjust? I mean this sort of thing, for example: everyone would surely agree that if a man borrows weapons from a sane friend, and if he goes mad and asks for them back, the friend should not return them, and would not be just if he did. Nor should anyone be willing to tell the whole truth to someone in such a state.”

  • Cephalus: “That’s true.”

  • Socrates: “Then the following is not the definition of justice: to speak the truth and repay what one has borrowed.” (p. 77/331b-d)

  • Polemarchus interjects that this is the correct definition. Cephalus hands over the reins of the conversation to Polemarchus.

Second Definition of Justice – Polemarchus

  • Polemarchus modifies Cephalus’ definition: Justice is giving benefit to one’s friends, and giving harm to one’s enemies. (p. 78)

    • Polemarchus argues that this is what is truly owed to one’s friends and enemies, respectively.

  • Socrates raises a number of problems with Polemarchus’ definition (pp. 79-81)

    • Should you benefit those who you believe to be your friends (who seem to be good, but aren’t), or those who are truly your friends (who are truly good)?

      • Polemarchus initially says the former. But Socrates responds that if those you believe to be your friends are actually bad people (i.e. unjust people, not really your friends), then justice would mean benefitting those who are unjust, and harming those who are just. But this is absurd.

      • Polemarchus changes his mind to the latter. But Socrates responds that harming anyone can only stand to make them worse, whereas justice should promote the good of those acted towards justly. Thus justice cannot involve harming anyone. (A tricky argument!)

  • Socrates: “All right. Since it has become apparent, then, that neither justice nor the just consists in benefiting friends and harming enemies, what else should one say it is?” (p. 81/336a)

Third Definition of Justice – Thrasymachus(p. 81-93/336a-354c)

  • Thrasymachus intervenes like a “wild beast about to spring,” chastising Socrates for refusing to offer his own answer to the question “What is justice?”

    • “What nonsense you two have been talking all this time, Socrates! Why do you act like naïve people, giving way to one another? If you really want to know what justice is, don’t just ask questions and then indulge your love of honor by refuting the answers.”

  • But Socrates manages to convince Thrasymachus to share his own opinion. (p. 81-82)

  • Thrasymachus: “I say justice is nothing other than what is advantageous for the stronger.” (p. 82/338b-c)

    • In other words, in every society there is a ‘stronger’ or more powerful individual, group, or class, which sets the rules and laws, for the sake of their own advantage. They call these rules or laws ‘justice’. But this is just a fancy word to describe the rules that the powerful have created for their own benefit, and which they require the weaker to obey. [A common view even today?]

    • [But this is really just to say that there is no such thing as justice...]

  • (Recall: Thrasymachus is a sophist – a paid teacher of rhetoric and oratory.)

    • For Plato, the sophists are the ‘enemies’ or ‘foils’ of the philosophers.

Socrates’ Objection #1 to Thrasymachus(p. 83-84/339b-341b)

  • Objection #1: The stronger qua ruler can be in error about what is advantageous for themselves. Yet the weaker remain obligated to obey that which the stronger/ruler mistakenlybelieves to be to their advantage, and this is still called ‘justice’. In such a case, justice would be what is disadvantageous to the stronger.

  • Thrasymachus’ Reply #1: Justice is not what the stronger believe to be advantageous for themselves, but only what the stronger are correct about being advantageous for themselves.

    • [Socrates’ point: Justice must involve correct knowledge/wisdom.]

    Socrates’ Objection #2 to Thrasymachus(p. 84-86/341c-343c)

    • Objection #2: Justice, as Thrasymachus agrees, is the craft or expertise of ruling cities. But consider some analogies: The expertise of medicine is concerned with what is advantageous for the body of the patient, not the doctor. The expertise of shepherding is concerned with what is advantageous for the sheep, not the shepherd. So the expertise of justice should be concerned with what is advantageous for the city and its citizens, not the ruler.

      • In other words, justice must be what is advantageous for the weaker, not the stronger.

      • Thrasymachus again gets angry, asking Socrates if he still has a “wet nurse”, since he seems to be ‘running around snivelling’ and needs to ‘wipe his nose’.

    • Thrasymachus’ Reply #2: Shepherds may look after the advantage of the sheep, but only because this ultimately serves the shepherd’s own advantage.

      • (Socrates will later respond: But in order for the shepherd to receive advantage for themselves, they must bring advantage to their sheep. Likewise, a doctor may receive payment – a form of advantage – through their practice of medicine, but only if they use their expertise in medicine for the advantage of the patient.)

    Thrasymachus’ Definition Re-Stated(p. 86/343d-344c)

    • Thrasymachus now states his view of justice differently: In truth, injustice is most advantageous for oneself. (p. 86)

      • The unjust person reaps far more benefits than the just person (as long as theydon’t get caught).

        • An unjust person can cheat, steal, lie, defraud, etc., in order to benefit themselves. The just person can’t. So the unjust person is better off and happier than the just person.

      • The person with the greatest advantage (and thus the happiest person) is therefore the tyrant, who perpetrates the most complete injustice – namely, tyranny.

    Plato, Republic, Book IX Against Tyranny

    And amid evils such as these will not he who is ill-governed in his own person - the tyrannical man, I mean - whom you just now decided to be the most miserable of all - will not he be yet more miserable when, instead of leading a private life, he is constrained by fortune to be a public tyrant? He has to be master of others when he is not master of himself: he is like a diseased or paralytic man who is compelled to pass his life, not in retirement, but fighting and combating with other men.

    Yes, he said, the similitude is most exact.

    Is not his case utterly miserable? and does not the actual tyrant lead a worse life than he whose life you determined to be the worst?

    Certainly.

    He who is the real tyrant, whatever men may think, is the real slave, and is obliged to practise the greatest adulation and servility, and to be the flatterer of the vilest of mankind. He has desires which he is utterly unable to satisfy, and has more wants than any one, and is truely poor, if you know how to inspect the whole soul of him: all his life long he is beset with fear and is full of convulsions and distractions, even as the State which he resembles: and surely the resemblance holds?

    Vert true, he said.

    Moreover, as we were saying before, he grows worse from having power: he becomes and is of necessity more jealous, more faithless, more unjust, more friendless, more impious, than he was at first; he is the purveyor and cherisher of every sort of vice, and the consequence is that he is supremely miserable, and that he makes everybody else as miserable as himself.

    Socrates’ Objection(s) #3 to Thrasymachus(p. 89-92/348b-352b)

    • Thrasymachus says that injustice is good, wise, prudent, and virtuous. But people good and wise with respect to a given area of expertise do not seek to get the better of others sharing that expertise, insofar as they practice it (e.g. a doctor does not seek to beat out other doctors, but rather wants all to practice medicine well). Yet an unjust person wants to get the better of other unjust people, for their own advantage. This seems to suggest that injustice is a kind badness or ignorance, not good or wisdom. [This is a subtle point....]

      • (Thrasymachus begins to sweat and blush.)

    • Even criminals [e.g. the mafia] or tyrants are obliged to behave justly with one another if they are going to be successful in ruling or exerting power over others. Injustice among themselves will lead to quarrels, conflict, factions, etc. – nothing will get done.

    Socrates’ Objection #4 to Thrasymachus: The Function Argument (p. 92-93/352d-354a)

    • Everything has a function. The function of the eye is to see. The function of a knife is to cut.

    • Something is good if it performs its function well. A good eye is an eye that sees well. A good knife is a knife that cuts well. Etc.

    • The virtue of something is that which makes it able to perform its function well. The virtue of the eye is sight (for that allows it to see well). The virtue of a knife is sharpness (for that allows it to cut well). Etc.

    • The function of the soul (psyche) is “taking care of things, ruling, deliberating, and all other such things” – or in general, living.

      • Thus the soul has a virtue through which it can live and take care of things well.

      • Justice is this virtue of the soul through which it can take care of things, rule, and deliberate well.

      • So the just person will live well, and the unjust person will live badly

      • “And surely anyone who lives well is blessed and happy, and anyone who does not is the opposite.”

      • “Therefore, a just person is happy and an unjust one wretched.”

      • “But surely it is profitable, not to be wretched, but to be happy.”

      • “So then, blessed Thrasymachus, injustice is never more profitable than justice.”

    End of Book 1

    • Socrates has argued that injustice is never more profitable than justice.

    • Thrasymachus: “Let that be your banquet, Socrates, at the feast of Bendis.”

      • [The Republic is set during the festival of Bendis, goddess of the hunt.]

    • Socrates’ last words in Book 1: “Given by you, Thrasymachus, after you became gentle with me and ceased to be difficult. Yet I have not had a good banquet. But that is my fault, not yours. I seem to have behaved like those gluttons who snatch at every dish that passes and taste it before having properly savored the preceding one. Before finding the first thing we inquired about – namely, what justice is – I let that go, and turned to investigate whether it is a kind of vice and ignorance or a kind of wisdom and virtue. Then an argument came up about injustice being more profitable than justice, and I could not refrain from abandoning the previous one and following up on it. Hence the result of the discussion, so far as I am concerned, is that I know nothing. For when I do not know what justice is, I will hardly know whether it is a kind of virtue or not, or whether a person who has it is happy or unhappy.” (p. 93/354a-c)

      • [Book 1 ends like the Euthyphro: without answering “What is x?”]

    • Reflection on Sophistry and Philosophy:
