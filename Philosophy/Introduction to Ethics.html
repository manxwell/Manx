Part 1. Ethics in Ancient Philosophy

Plato, Euthyphro

Socrates's Question:

What is piety, and what is impiety?

i.e. What is virtue?

Euthyphro's Answer:

Piety, then is that which is dear to the gods, and impiety is that which is not dear to them.

i.e. Virtue is what the gods love.

Socrates's Question (explanatory priority):

The point which I should first wish to understand is whether the pious or holy is beloved by the gods because it is holy, or holy because it is beloved of the gods.

i.e. Are virtuous acts virtuous because the gods love them, or do the gods love them because they are virtuous?

Euthyphro's Answer:

Gods love them because they are virtuous.

Socrates:

$\dots$ you apear to me, Euthyphro, when I ask you what is essence of holiness, to offer an attribute only, and not the essence $\dots$ tell me once more what holiness or piety really is, whether dear to the gods or not $\dots$ and what is impiety?

i.e. Your answer doesn't explain anything.

Euthyphro:

Great, I'm confused.

Plato, Apology

The old Accusations

  1. Socrates uses tricks to win arguments,
  2. discusses trivial and impractical topics,
  3. and is a teacher

The New Accusations

  1. Socrates is the only person that corrupts the youth of Athens, and he did this willingly,

    2. Socrates' response (An Argument by Analogy)

    Educating youth is like training horses.

    Very few people know how to train horses well. Most who try teach them bad habits.

    Likewise, the majority of people don't know how to educate youth, and only a select few have the knowledge and skills to make them better people.

    So Socrates cannot be the only person in Athens who corrupts the youth. However, he may be one of the few that knows how to make them better!

    Second argument, that Socrates didn't corrupt the youth of Athens

    • If Socrates corrupted anyone, he either corrupted willingly or he corrupted unwillingly.

      No one willingly corrupts those near them; that's self-destructive.

      Therefore, if Socrates corrupted then he must have done so unwillingly.(by disjunctive syllogism)

    • But if Socrates corrupted unwillingly, the law demands that he be instructed not to corrupt (and not punished)

      No one tried to instruct Socrates or warn him he was doing harm.

      Therefore, Socrates didn't corrupt unwillingly (by modus tollens)

    This leads to a contradiction, hence Socrates didn't corrupt the youth of Athens.

    Third argument, that Socrates didn't corrupt the youth of Athens

    If Socrates corrupted the youth, there would be witnesses.

    No witnesses came forward.

    Therefore, Socrates didn't corrupt the youth (by modus tollens)

  2. and is a total athiest

    3. Socrates' response

    If Socrates believes in spiritual things, then he believes in gods.

    Socrates believes in spirtual things e.g. like the Oracle at Delphi.

    Socrates believes in gods. (by modus ponens)

The new accusations are more dangerous but refutable.
